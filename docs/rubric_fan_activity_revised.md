# Fan Activity: Autoethnographic Exercise Rubric

**Total: 150 points**

| Criteria | Excellent | Good | Developing | Needs Work | Points |
|---|---|---|---|---:|
| **Substantive Fan Participation** | Participates in a clearly meaningful fan activity involving real engagement with fans, creators, events, or fan spaces. The activity provides rich material for analysis. | Participates in a relevant fan activity, though the engagement may be somewhat limited or uneven. | Participation is present but thin, mostly passive, or only minimally connected to fandom. | Activity is missing, unclear, not substantive, or not clearly related to fandom. | 30 |
| **Documentation of Activity** | Provides clear, appropriate documentation of participation, such as screenshots, photos, submitted letters/emails, badge images, or other evidence. Documentation supports the analysis effectively. | Provides adequate documentation, though it may be limited, incomplete, or only loosely connected to the written analysis. | Provides minimal documentation, or documentation is difficult to interpret. | Documentation is missing or does not demonstrate participation. | 20 |
| **Description and Use of Concrete Detail** | Clearly describes the setting, interaction, atmosphere, key moments, and specific details. Description is vivid and selective, providing evidence for later analysis. | Describes the activity clearly, though some details may be general or underdeveloped. | Description is vague, overly brief, or mostly summary without enough concrete detail. | Description is missing, confusing, or too general to support analysis. | 25 |
| **Reflexivity and Positionality** | Thoughtfully addresses the student’s position in relation to the fandom, including expectations, insider/outsider status, identity, experience level, and how their presence shaped the experience. | Addresses positionality, though the reflection may be somewhat general or not fully integrated into the analysis. | Mentions positionality only briefly or superficially. | Does not meaningfully address positionality or reflexivity. | 25 |
| **Autoethnographic Analysis and Interpretation** | Moves beyond personal reaction to interpret what the experience reveals about fandom. Analyzes norms, belonging, exclusion, gatekeeping, hierarchy, commerce, performance, identity, expertise, or other relevant dynamics. | Offers some interpretation of fandom, though analysis may remain uneven or partly descriptive. | Analysis is limited, mostly focused on personal opinion, or does not clearly explain what the experience reveals about fandom. | Little or no analysis; reads mainly as a diary entry, reaction post, or event report. | 30 |
| **Connection to Course Concepts** | Makes clear, accurate, and useful connections to course concepts such as participatory culture, fan communities, commercialization, identity, or fan labor. | Refers to course concepts, though connections may be brief, broad, or not fully developed. | Mentions course concepts only in passing or without clear explanation. | Does not connect the experience to course concepts. | 15 |
| **Organization, Style, and Mechanics** | Well organized, polished, and within the expected 500–750 word range. Writing is clear, coherent, and mostly free of distracting errors. | Generally clear and organized, with minor issues in structure, style, length, or mechanics. | Organization or writing issues sometimes interfere with clarity; may be noticeably too short or too long. | Difficult to follow, poorly organized, or contains frequent errors that interfere with meaning. | 5 |

## Grading Scale

| Points | Grade Range |
|---:|---|
| 135–150 | Excellent |
| 120–134 | Good |
| 105–119 | Developing |
| 0–104 | Needs Work |

## Brief Description of Expectations

A strong submission does more than report that a fan activity happened. It uses the student’s own participation as evidence for thinking critically about fandom. The best essays balance concrete description, self-reflection, and broader analysis. Students should explain not only what they did and what they observed, but also what their experience reveals about fan communities, norms, identities, hierarchies, and practices.
