## Online Discussion: Data Modeling for Comics and Fandom

**Readings:**
- Trevor Owens, “Defining Data for Humanists: Text, Artifact, Information or Evidence?” (2011)
- Christof Schöch, “Big? Smart? Clean? Messy? Data in the Humanities” (2013)
- Karen Sosulski, “The Data” (2019)
- Lauren Shroff, “Datasets as Imagination” (2023)
- John A. Walsh et al., “The Personal Library and presumed reading of Algernon Charles Swinburne” (2025)

**Assignment (Reading Response, Canvas Post)**

Before class, complete the assigned readings carefully. As you read, take notes and mark passages, concepts, or arguments that you find especially interesting, challenging, or generative.

Before the session, post a **2–3 paragraph response** (approximately 300–400 words) to Canvas. Your post should present a **specific, original reflection** grounded in the readings and written in clear academic prose (introductory sentence, coherent development, concluding insight).

### Prompt

This week’s readings ask us to think critically about **what counts as data**, especially in humanities contexts. Rather than treating data as something simply given or collected, the readings emphasize that data is selected, structured, cleaned, interpreted, and presented. Taken together, they encourage us to see datasets not as neutral containers of information, but as constructed representations shaped by scholarly choices, methods, and assumptions.

Choose **one key idea, tension, or claim** from the readings that you find especially productive for thinking about comics, fandom, or humanities research more broadly.

Briefly explain how the author frames this issue, then use a **specific example** to reflect on how some humanities object, artifact, or body of evidence might be transformed into data. Your example may come from comics, fandom, or another domain you know well.

You might consider questions such as:

- How do scholarly choices shape the transformation of comics or fandom materials into a dataset?
- What is gained—and what is lost—when cultural materials are turned into structured datasets?
- How do the categories, fields, or metadata in a dataset shape what kinds of questions can be asked?
- What makes humanities data “messy,” and how should scholars respond to that messiness?
- In what sense might a dataset reflect imagination, interpretation, or argument rather than simply record facts?
- What does a **data paper** make visible that a dataset alone does not?

You may also reflect briefly on how thinking about comics or fandom as data might change the kinds of evidence scholars value, the kinds of patterns they can identify, or the kinds of arguments they are able to make.
